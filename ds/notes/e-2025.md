### G1

#### 1. Assessing software quality often involves analyzing multiple criteria and presenting them in a way that supports informed evaluation and decision-making

##### a. Indicate one method that can be used for multi-criteria optimization and explain what is consists of
>
>Some methods to measure software quality in a multi-criteria scenario include: Pareto Charts, that states that roughly 80% of the effects come from 20% of the causes or the Star Plot, that uses a star-like diagram to represent multiple variables. Both methods help to visualize and analyze the trade-offs between different quality attributes, allowing decision-makers to identify the most critical factors affecting software quality.

##### b. How does the method identified in (a) help make a comparative analysis of conflicting aspects?
>
>The Pareto Charts helps with comparative analysis by allowing the identification of the most significant factors among a set of factors. By focusing on the critical few causes that contribute to the majority of the effects, decision-makers can prioritize their efforts and resources effectively.

#### 2. Availability and reliability are key quality attributes for software systems

##### a. Explain what availability consists of and how it differs from reliability
>
>Availability is the degree to which a system is operational and accessible when required for use. It is often expressed as a percentage of uptime over a specified period. Reliability however is the degree to which a system performs its intended functions without failure over a specified period under specified conditions.

##### a. Suggest an architectural style suitable for this system, considering the need for scalability parallel processing, and independent evolution of inspection functionalities. Describe, in abstract terms, the types of components and connectors used
>
>A pipe-filter architecture could be used, as it allows for data to be processed in a series of independent stages (filters) connected by data streams (pipes). Each filter can perform a specific transformation on the data, making it easy to add, remove, or modify processing steps without affecting the overall system. It can be expanded by parallelizing filters or adding new filters to the pipeline. Some filters can be used for virus scanning, others for link analysis, and others for content extraction. The final filter decides whether to store or discard the email based on the results of the previous filters, and an extra filter handles user notifications.

#### 2. Identify one architectural decision in the proposed solution that improves modifiability and briefly explain why

>The use of pipe interfaces allows for easy integration of new filters into the existing architecture. Each filter operates independently and communicates with others through well-defined interfaces (pipes). #### 3 Imagine that the system needs to incorporate a new email analysis capability, such as detecting phishing attempts based on email content and sender behavior. How would the architecture identified in 1 (a) support the introduction of this new functionality?
>
>An extra filter/pipe can be added to handle phishing attempt filtering by simply inserting it between the existing filters, without affecting the other components. This filter would analyze the email content for common phishing indicators and either flag or discard suspicious emails before they reach the storage or notification stages. In other architectures, such as monolithic or tightly coupled systems, adding new functionality would require significant changes to the existing codebase, potentially introducing bugs and increasing maintenance complexity.

##### b. What are the advantages of managing MailGuard as a Software Product Line instead of developing independent email-protection systems for each configuration?
>
>Allows for code reuse and reduced development costs, as the same core components can be used across multiple products with only minor modifications needed for each variant, where ach configuration is a separate product, which can be tailored to specific customer needs while leveraging a common codebase. Consistent quality and testing, as core components are developed and tested once, reducing the likelihood of defects across different product variants. Faster time-to-market, as new product variants can be developed more quickly by reusing existing components and focusing on configuration rather than building from scratch. Economies of scale in maintenance and support, as updates and bug fixes can be applied to the core components and automatically benefit all product variants. Flexible for each customer needs. Systematic reuse and consistency of architecture. Knowledge retention within the organization, as best practices and design patterns are encapsulated in the core components, facilitating knowledge transfer among development teams.
